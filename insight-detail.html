Why the "creator" label doesn't fit B2B — and what does.

By the Credible team · Published 12 June 2026 · 8 min read

01

The word creator is doing too much work. When a B2B marketing team says they want to work with creators, they usually mean something quite specific — and quite different from what a consumer brand means when they say the same thing.

We spent the first half of the year running a research programme with 42 in-house creator marketing leads at B2B companies. The message came back consistently: the vocabulary is broken. The people they want to work with aren't influencers. They aren't UGC. They aren't lifestyle voices. They're experts — operators, founders, investors and specialists who publish.

"The word creator makes my CFO think of TikTok. It makes my customers think of TikTok. It's the wrong association at every step of the internal sell."

So what should we call them? We've been using the term Expert Creator internally at Credible for two years, and we've concluded it does the job. Not because it's linguistically elegant — it isn't — but because it forces the distinction into every conversation.

The five differences that matter

If you're a buyer trying to explain to your leadership why you're spending budget on this creator and not that one, these are the five distinctions worth naming:

  • Where the credibility comes from. Experts earned it from doing the work. Influencers earned it from platform-native content.
  • What the audience does for a living. Expert Creators' audiences are practitioners in their own field, not aspirational fans.
  • What "engagement" means. A save on a LinkedIn post from a CFO is worth more than 10,000 likes on Reels.
  • The relationship to platforms. Experts publish across many; influencers optimise for one.
  • What kind of deal they'll take. Experts pass on 80% of inbound. Influencers negotiate on 80%.

Why this matters for buyers

The category confusion isn't just cosmetic. It changes how the deal gets structured, who signs off internally, which team owns the outcome, and how the work gets measured. If you treat an Expert Creator like an influencer, you'll get an influencer's output.

The Expert Creator taxonomy also changes how the deal gets bought. Most companies buying influence work today have a marketing budget for it. Most companies buying Expert Creator work are drawing from PR, content, thought leadership, events, or a new dedicated creator marketing budget. Those are five different signatures on five different POs.

What we do with this

At Credible we sign a specific type of Expert Creator, in one of four archetypes: Founders/C-Suite, Subject Matter Experts, Investors/Analysts, and Category Specialists. Every representation decision is made against that framework.

And every buyer conversation starts with the same short sentence: we manage experts who publish, not influencers who happen to work in B2B.

The rest of the conversation goes better from there.
